Background & Context
Steelhead overshoot · ESA listing · Study area · Hypotheses
Background

The Steelhead Overshoot Phenomenon

Adult steelhead migrating upstream pass their natal tributary before attempting to return — a phenomenon that has become pervasive in the Columbia River Basin.

  • Some Upper Columbia populations: >50% overshoot rates
  • 8–9 mainstem dams alter thermal & hydraulic conditions
  • Warm water temps drive fish to seek coldwater refugia upstream
  • Overshoot probability falls from >90% → <25% as temp rises 10°→20°C
  • Ladder placement matters: opposite-shore ladders → 18% more overshoots
  • Hatchery fish overshoot 15–65% more than wild fish
Wells Dam (RK 830) had the 2nd highest overshoot rate in the Upper Columbia — approximately 20% of passing wild steelhead were overshoot fish (Murdoch et al. 2022)

What Happens After Overshoot?

~60%
Fall back downstream toward natal tributaries
~40%
Fail to return downstream (Priest Rapids; Murdoch 2022)

Critical Gap

Only 3% of wild overshoot fish were detected on Upper Columbia spawning grounds — representing significant population-level mortality

Background

Conservation Significance

ESA Status

  • Originally listed as Endangered — 1997
  • Reclassified to Threatened — 2006
  • Upper Columbia Spring-run Chinook & Steelhead Recovery Plan (2007)
  • 306 recovery actions identified

Harvest Context

  • Mark-selective fisheries: only adipose-clipped hatchery fish may be retained
  • ESA impact limit: 2% for most wild stocks
  • Harvest open: 2000–2015, 2024–2025
  • Emergency closure: 2016–2023 (low wild returns)
  • ~50–65% of hatchery fish are adipose-clipped (harvestable)

Why Overshoot Fate Matters

  • ~40% of wild steelhead passing Priest Rapids Dam are overshoot fish
  • 40% of those fail to return downstream
  • Overshoot mortality is a direct drag on population productivity
  • Understanding drivers is essential for recovery planning
This study: First systematic evaluation of whether dam spill conditions and harvest exposure predict post-overshoot fate at Wells Dam over a 20-year period.
Background

Study Area & Detection Framework

Wells Dam — River Kilometer 830

  • Located upstream of Wenatchee & Entiat natal tributaries
  • Any Wenatchee or Entiat basin origin steelhead detected at Wells has overshot its destination
  • Okanogan and Methow Rivers are above Wells (non-natal basins)
  • PIT-tag antenna arrays above, at, and below dam

Data Sources

  • PTAGIS — PIT-tag detection records 2001–2025
  • Columbia River DART — Hourly spill data 2000–2024
  • WDFW regulations — Annual harvest status

Detection Zone Classification

Below — downstream of Wells → fallback success
At Wells — last detected at dam → presumed failure
Above — continued to Okanogan/Methow (non-natal)

Spill Year Assignment

June 1 cutoff: fish detected between June Y and May Y+1 → assigned to Spill Year Y+1. This pairs each fish with the fall conditions during arrival and spring conditions during holding.

Background

Study Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1 — Spill

Spill conditions at Wells Dam during the fall arrival period (Aug–Nov) or the subsequent spring holding period (Jan–Mar) influence the probability that overshoot steelhead return downstream toward natal tributaries.

Hypothesis 2 — Harvest Exposure

Hatchery steelhead detected during years when harvest is open are less likely to return downstream than other fish — either due to direct harvest mortality or behavioral differences between hatchery and wild fish.

Spill Variability

How the Model Accounts for Spill Variability

1 · Standardization

All spill metrics are z-scored (mean=0, SD=1) before entry. This places metrics with vastly different units on a common scale and prevents numerical instability in Stan/HMC. A β_spill coefficient = change in log-odds per one SD increase in spill.

Fall SD = 2,021 KCFS-hr · Spring SD = 4,738 KCFS-hr — so identical β values correspond to very different absolute spill amounts.

2 · Year Random Intercepts

α_j ~ Normal(μ_α, σ_α)

Year intercepts absorb all unmeasured year-level variation. If a high-spill year also had high return rates for other reasons (low density, favorable temperature), the random intercept captures that — preventing spurious attribution to spill.

3 · Wide Dynamic Range = Power

Fall TotalSpill ranged 13-fold. Spring TotalSpill ranged >12,000-fold. Despite this enormous variation providing statistical leverage, neither seasonal metric produced a credible nonzero effect. The null result is therefore robust — it was not a power problem.

4 · Weakly Informative Priors

β_spill ~ Normal(0, 1)

Allows effects up to OR ≈ 7.4 (β=2.0) while providing mild regularization toward zero. Spill coefficients remained near zero — data-driven, not prior-constrained. Harvest coefficient (β≈−2.3) easily overcame the prior.

σ_year = 0.49–0.66 across models — substantial unexplained inter-annual variation, confirming the random intercept structure is doing meaningful work.

Predictors: Spill & Harvest Exposure

Seasonal Spill Metrics (Year-Level)

  • Fall (Aug 1–Nov 30): Total KCFS-hours, spill hours, spill days
  • Spring (Jan 1–Mar 31): Same three metrics

HarvestExposure = 1 if:
  Rear Type = "H" (hatchery)

HarvestExposure = 0 if:

Analysis 1 · Methods

Bayesian Hierarchical Logistic Regression

Model Structure

y_ij ~ Bernoulli(p_ij)
logit(p_ij) = α_j + β_spill·Spill_j + β_harvest·Harvest_i
α_j ~ Normal(μ_α, σ_α)

y_ij = 1 if fish i in year j returned downstream

Weakly Informative Priors

μ_α ~ Normal(0, 2)
σ_α ~ HalfNormal(0, 1)
β_spill ~ Normal(0, 1)
β_harvest ~ Normal(0, 1)

MCMC Sampling

  • 4 chains × 4,500 iterations (1,500 warmup + 3,000 sampling)
  • 12,000 posterior samples per parameter
  • adapt_delta = 0.95 (minimize divergent transitions)

Convergence Criteria

  • R̂ (Gelman-Rubin) ≤ 1.01 — all models achieved
  • Bulk ESS > 3,000 — all fixed effects exceeded
  • Visual trace plot inspection — adequate chain mixing
Partial pooling: Year random intercepts α_j borrow strength across 20 years — years with very few fish (n=1–3) are regularized toward the population mean rather than being estimated with extreme uncertainty.

Analysis 2 · Rationale

Why Analyze Wild Fish Separately?

Biological Justification

  • Wild fish are protected under mark-selective regulations — not subject to direct harvest
  • Wild fish exhibit stronger homing fidelity than hatchery fish
  • Wild fish have 15–65% lower overshoot rates than hatchery fish
  • Wild fish survival is the ESA recovery target

Statistical Justification

  • Individual harvest exposure variable not applicable to wild fish
  • Year-level harvest season status is a different, weaker predictor
  • Wild fish have a 3-way fate that a binary model misses

Analysis 2 · Methods

Bayesian Hierarchical Multinomial Logistic Regression

Model Structure

y_ij ~ Categorical(p_ij)

log(p_Below / p_Wells) = α_j[B] + β_spill[B]·Spill_j + β_h[B]·HarvOpen_j

log(p_Above / p_Wells) = α_j[A] + β_spill[A]·Spill_j + β_h[A]·HarvOpen_j

α_j[k] ~ Normal(μ_α[k], σ_α[k])

Reference Category

Wells Dam = reference (the "failure" outcome). Two sets of log-odds contrasts are simultaneously estimated — one for downstream fallback, one for above-Wells migration — each relative to remaining at Wells.

Strengths & Limitations
What the analyses do well · Where caution is warranted
Evaluation

Analytical Strengths

Statistical Rigor

  • Bayesian hierarchical framework provides principled uncertainty quantification
  • Full posterior distributions — not just point estimates and p-values
  • Partial pooling across years handles sparse data years (n=1–3) without excluding them
  • MCMC convergence confirmed: R̂ ≤ 1.01, ESS > 3,000
  • Zero divergent transitions — no pathological geometry

Robustness Testing

  • 6 spill metrics tested — harvest result unchanged across all
  • Prior sensitivity analysis (4 specifications) — conclusions unchanged
  • Posterior predictive calibration: 17/17 years bracketed
  • LOO-CV: 0/208 influential observations

Study Design

  • 20-year dataset (2005–2024) with substantial spill variability
  • Individual-level harvest exposure predictor more precise than year-level indicator
  • Wild vs. hatchery separation provides mutual validation
  • Multinomial model for wild fish captures the full 3-way fate distribution
  • Null spill result against wide spill variation = high-power null

Triangulation

  • Two independent analyses converge on the same spill conclusion
  • Wild fish null harvest effect validates hatchery harvest effect as real
  • Consistent with prior literature (Murdoch 2022, Richins & Skalski 2018)
Evaluation

Limitations & Caveats

Detection-Based Inference

  • Fates inferred from last detection location, not direct observation
  • Fish last detected at Wells may have died, emigrated undetected, or been missed
  • Detection efficiency varies by site and infrastructure changes over time
  • No swim-path or acoustic telemetry to resolve ambiguous fates

Temporal Confounding

  • Harvest closure (2016–2023) is a contiguous block — impossible to fully separate harvest effect from other temporal trends
  • Environmental conditions, fish abundance, and management also changed during this period
  • Without randomized assignment, causal inference is limited

Sample Size Limitations

  • Wild fish analysis: only 208 fish across 17 years (avg. 12/year)
  • Year-level spill predictor has only 17–20 independent observations
  • Post-2021 years: n=1–3 fish each — very high uncertainty
  • Power to detect modest spill effects on wild fish is limited

Other Limitations

  • Above-Wells fate (9.1% wild) has indeterminate final outcome (stray vs. perished)
  • Spill metric may not capture hydraulic conditions fish actually experienced
  • Harvest mechanism unresolved: direct mortality vs. behavioral differences
  • Not all fish had rear type data — potential bias if missing non-random

Synthesis & Conclusions
Cross-analysis findings · Management implications · Future directions
Synthesis

What the Two Analyses Tell Us Together

Harvest Drives Fate

Hatchery fish during open harvest years had 90% lower odds of returning downstream. This is the dominant signal in 20 years of data.

Spill is Irrelevant

Neither fall nor spring spill predicted overshoot fate in either analysis — despite extreme interannual variation providing ample statistical leverage.

Wild Fish Do Better

Wild overshoot fish return downstream at 68.3% — more than double the rate in the full dataset — reflecting better homing and absence of harvest effects.

The mutual validation: Wild fish show NO response to harvest season status (expected, since they're protected), while hatchery fish show an overwhelming response to individual harvest exposure. This divergence is strong evidence that the hatchery harvest effect is real and causal, not a statistical artifact.
The spill story: Spill varied 13–12,000-fold over the study period. With this dynamic range, the analyses were well-powered. The consistent null result across 8 model specifications (6 binary + 2 multinomial) is compelling evidence that spill simply does not influence post-overshoot fallback success.

Unexplained Year Variation

σ_year = 0.49–0.66 in Analysis 1 and present in Analysis 2 — substantial inter-annual variation beyond spill and harvest. Likely contributors:

  • Water temperature affecting fish physiology and homing
  • Overall fish density and run abundance
  • Changes in PIT-tag detection infrastructure
  • Fish condition and ocean survival variation

Synthesis

The Spill Null Result: What It Means

The absence of a detectable spill effect is one of the most important findings — not because it is surprising, but because the analyses were well-powered to detect one.

Evidence Against a Spill Effect

  • 13-fold range in fall spill (724–9,667 KCFS-hr) — 20 independent year contrasts
  • 12,000-fold range in spring spill — extreme variation
  • 6 binary models (Analysis 1) all return β_spill ≈ 0
  • 2 multinomial models (Analysis 2) all return β_spill ≈ 0
  • Year random intercepts confirmed to absorb residual year variation
Conclusion: If spill had an effect of even moderate magnitude on overshoot fallback, it would have been detected in these data. The null result appears to be a genuine biological finding.

What Spill Might Still Affect

  • Probability of overshoot occurring in the first place (not evaluated here)
  • Timing of fallback (not evaluated)
  • Route selection during downstream passage
  • Survival during downstream passage through ladders/turbines

One Anomalous Finding

Fall SpillHours showed β = −0.30 with HDI excluding zero. However:

  • Not replicated by Fall SpillDays or Fall TotalSpill
  • Sign reverses between fall and spring SpillHours
  • Likely reflects collinearity or metric-specific confounding
  • Not interpreted as a genuine biological effect
Synthesis

Management Implications

1 · Harvest Management

Harvest exposure is strongly associated with reduced downstream detection of overshoot steelhead. Managers should factor overshoot fish dynamics into harvest impact assessments. The return to open harvest in 2024–2025 warrants close monitoring.

2 · Spill Operations

Modifying fall or spring spill operations at Wells Dam is unlikely to improve downstream return rates for overshoot fish. Spill optimization for overshoot adult fallback is not supported by these data.

3 · Wild Fish Monitoring

Wild overshoot fish are a relatively resilient subgroup (68.3% fallback). The 22.6% that remain at Wells without detection and the 9.1% detected in non-natal basins above Wells warrant continued monitoring — particularly any above-Wells stray spawning.

4 · Unresolved Year Variation

Substantial unexplained year-to-year variation (σ_year ≈ 0.5–0.7) suggests additional drivers beyond spill and harvest. Temperature, flow, and fish condition deserve systematic evaluation in future analyses.

Synthesis

Future Research Directions

Resolve the Harvest Mechanism

  • Integrate creel survey and harvest encounter data with PIT-tag records
  • Use acoustic or radio telemetry to track individual fish fate through the harvest area
  • Quantify actual overshoot fish harvest rates vs. behavioral dropout rates

Evaluate Temperature & Condition

  • Incorporate daily water temperature at Wells Dam as a covariate
  • Assess fish condition indices (ocean survival, body size) as predictors
  • Evaluate the thermal refuge hypothesis for overshoot occurrence

Extend the Wild Fish Analysis

  • Grow the wild fish dataset — currently only 208 fish over 17 years
  • Investigate what happens to the 9.1% that migrate above Wells
  • Are above-Wells fish straying to spawn in Okanogan/Methow?
  • What is their ultimate fate and recovery relevance?

Overshoot Occurrence Models

  • Evaluate spill and thermal effects on the probability of overshoot occurring
  • Separate overshoot occurrence from post-overshoot fate in a joint model
Conclusions

Key Conclusions

1
Harvest exposure is the primary driver of steelhead overshoot fate at Wells Dam. Hatchery fish in open harvest years had ~90% lower odds of being detected returning downstream (β = −2.28, OR = 0.10, P(β<0) = 1.000). This effect dwarfs all other predictors.
2
Neither fall nor spring spill conditions have a detectable effect on downstream return probability in either analysis. Despite 13–12,000-fold variation in spill providing high statistical power, consistent null results were found across 8 model variants.
3
Wild overshoot steelhead fall back at substantially higher rates (68.3%) than the full dataset (28.8%), and their fate is unaffected by harvest season status — consistent with mark-selective protections working as intended.
4
Substantial unexplained inter-annual variation (σ_year = 0.49–0.66) persists beyond spill and harvest. Temperature, flow, and fish condition represent important avenues for future research.
5
The complementary analytical framework — binary hierarchical for all fish, multinomial for wild fish — provides mutual validation and captures the full behavioral diversity of overshoot steelhead at Wells Dam.
